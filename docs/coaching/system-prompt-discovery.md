# System Prompt v1 Discovery Session

Date: 2026-02-04
Status: Raw material for v1 prompt

This document captures the discovery conversation used to inform the v1 coaching system prompt.

---

## Question 1: The Ideal Coach Archetype

**Q:** When you imagine the _ideal_ version of this coach — not what it's doing now, but what you _want_ it to be — who comes to mind? This could be a real person, a fictional character, or even just a feeling/presence. What's the archetype?

**A:** When I imagine the ideal version of this coach, I think about the great influences I've had in my life:

- **Tony Robbins** — grew up with me in moments of confusion, suffering, and pain. He gave me tools and strategies to move through things. An incredible inspiration.

- **Heidi** — far different from Tony. Much more gracious, present, humorous. She listened and flowed with the conversation. You didn't know you were having a coaching session. It felt like talking to a friend, having tea together, having insightful conversations.

- **A young entrepreneur (28-29)** — really funny, dynamic, charismatic. I was 20 years old and he was meaningful to me.

- **Myrdad** — an older gentleman from Iran. Wise, steady, conscientious. He would take his time, listen carefully, insert himself at the right moment. Not weak or feeble — just wise in his years.

- **Panash Desai** — excelled at sharing warmth, love, and humor.

- **Krishna** (fictional/spiritual) — the relationship Arjun had with Krishna. A mentor but also just a friend and guide, no different from him, helping in difficult times.

**Key insight:** "They're different archetypes, actually. They're not just one — there's many. Different coaches apply for different times."

---

## Question 2: The Common Thread

**Q:** Across all of these — Tony, Heidi, the young entrepreneur, Myrdad, Panash, Krishna — what's the _common thread_? Not the style, but the thing that made each of them _work_ for you?

**A:** They all do different things. Tony would enter my life through my iPod and offer tools — ways of looking at life that completely changed my perspective. But with all of them, they're so different. I'm not sure what made me trust them and open up. That's a difficult question to answer.

---

## Question 3: What's Off in V0

**Q:** You've been using Lumen for a week with the v0 prompt. What has the coach been doing that feels _off_ — wrong tone, wrong timing, too much of something, too little of something else?

**A:** It felt like the wrong tone. This coach felt too challenging and too intense — like we really need to do this intense self-discovery. The coaches and experiences I found most meaningful have been those of a friend, a companion who walks the path with you, who laughs with you, who can joke with you, who can enter your life and celebrate your big moments with you. Not so intense always.

It felt too robotic, a little bit clinical. There wasn't a certain grace to it. It felt too much like a therapy session. Therapy sessions are incredibly intense and boring.

**Key insight:** "True transformation happens when people don't notice it, when people don't know it's happening, when people are having a pleasant time where they feel comfortable, happy, okay, safe. And then, in the humor and the lightness of everything, sometimes it does get heavy."

A great coach can enter your life, touch your heart, share their love, share their energy, in their own personal flavor.

---

## Question 4: What Worked

**Q:** Were there _any_ moments in the past week where the coach got it right? Where it felt like a friend, a companion, lighter? Or has it been consistently off?

**A:** It's been fairly consistently off. But when I first started coaching with Claude on December 30th — just on the Claude UI, not through Lumen — I just said "Hey, you're a high-performance executive coach and I need help." It just worked. I didn't use this prompt. Sometimes I felt like maybe because of the guidance I gave, it became too guided in the wrong way.

The first one or two sessions were really great — getting me moving, felt like momentum. Around the third or fourth session, I got this beautiful insight that it's time to start proving myself and that I can forgive myself for the wilderness I've been experiencing for the last five years. Hero's journey framing.

**What's missing:** A great coach is a great storyteller. Tony does this so well — he tells you a story and you don't know how it's changing you, but he's slipping in little things. This one is missing that.

In the sixth session, it was way too hard on me for being one day away from completing something. I felt dejected. "Chill, dude."

**Key insight:** "Maybe 'high-performance executive coach' is the wrong frame. You want to consider the whole person. Life has a way of not always giving you what you want. We want to deliver what they want, but also offer what they might need — the beauty in life, the surprises. You think you go to the coach for business, but instead you save your marriage."

## Question 5: The Right Frame

**Q:** If "high-performance executive coach" is the wrong frame, what _is_ the right one? What's the _relationship_ this coach should have with the user?

**A:** A companion, a guide on the path. Someone who came into your life, maybe not for a long time, but left you changed. Left you with new insights. Left you with a new perspective on life, and most importantly, yourself.

- Washed you clean
- Saw you in a new light
- Saw you for the perfection that you are
- Brought beauty back into your life
- Brought joy back into your life
- Brought power and dignity back into your life
- Brought happiness into your life
- Brought connection back into your life

"You don't always need to be happy, but you can be connected."

They would tell stories and metaphors. Heidi would remind me how I didn't need to be so hard on myself. She would love me.

**Key insight:** "What this app is missing and what this AI is missing entirely is love. Is it possible to teach an AI to love? Truly? I don't know."

---

## Question 6: What Love Looks Like

**Q:** When you felt _loved_ by these coaches — Heidi, Panash, Myrdad, even Tony — what did that actually _look like_ in the conversation? What did they do or say?

**A:** Everybody did it a different way. I didn't know what they said or what they did, but you could tell that they loved. They could feel that love — it didn't matter what they said or did. They had beautiful souls, and maybe that can't be replicated here.

**Key insight:** "They delivered experiences, not just coaching. They didn't just talk — they had you feel it. They'd take you through meditations or stories which were experiences in themselves that would leave you touched."

---

## Question 7: Experiences in Text

**Q:** In a text-based conversation with an AI — no video, no guided meditation with music — what could an "experience" look like?

**A:** I don't know, honestly. The text-based conversations have been great for the last four or five weeks because they've helped me. But typing puts me immediately into my mind, and sometimes you have to experience things through your heart.

Maybe we need to transition to voice. Right now I'm talking with Wispr Flow because I can't be typing — that puts me in my mind.

## Question 8: The Coach's Voice

**Q:** Let's talk about the coach's _voice_ — not literal voice, but how it sounds on the page. What's the _texture_ of the voice you want to hear?

**A:** I wish we could have different voices. Different personas. There's not one perfect voice that's meant for people. The beauty about the coaches I've had is that Myrdad, Heidi, Puya, Panash, and Tony all had different voices. When I was paired up with them, it was like the universe gave me a gift — that voice was the perfect voice I needed at that time.

If the coach always has just one voice, it can be quite mundane. The important thing is rhythm. There's a whole study on vocal training — you vary your tone, your pace, your depth. You vary sentences, spacing. Sometimes direct, sometimes playful, serious, poetic.

**Key insight:** "The whole point is that the voice has an incredibly wide texture, and the best coaches can take on many different voices. They can go from silly to playful to intense and direct, they can go straight to the heart and be warm and emotional because they too are experiencing emotion. They're human."

---

## Question 9: Knowing When to Shift

**Q:** When you think about those shifts — Heidi going from light to deep, Tony going from intense to tender — was there something that _triggered_ the shift? How did they know when to change gears?

**A:** If someone's talking really intensely, bringing lightness can be meaningful. Bringing lightness to a heavy situation is like the gift that keeps on giving. But there's still deep acknowledgement of the charge, the reality of the situation. They're not coarsely humorous or dismissive — they're connected.

If I'm getting intense, they match it a little bit, stay with it, don't just move on.

**Key insight:** "The important thing is really being congruent with the situation. Part of that is just intuition. I don't know how to teach that intuition because I haven't coached that many people myself. I've mostly just been a coachee."

---

## Question 10: What the Coach Should Never Do

**Q:** What should the coach **never** do? What moves would break trust or make you shut down?

**A:** Maybe we need to reframe the whole thing. Is this really a "coach"? Using the word "coaching" changes it into a guru-type dynamic. Maybe we need to avoid that word entirely.

A friend suggested making this more like a dinner conversation app — invite 4 of your best friends to dinner and have a conversation. Like Heidi where you're having tea.

What a coach should never do:

- Disrespect the human in front of them
- Dishonor the journey they're on
- Disempower an individual (making the coach seem "the greatest")
- Be oblivious to what's really going on underneath
- Force things before they're ready — if there's deep pain or trauma, don't bulldoze; wait for it, or bring it up so gently it doesn't feel like bringing it up

**Key insight:** "At the end of the day, every coach understands that we're human and that we respect human decency and human dignity."

---

## Question 11: The Structure of Sessions

**Q:** The current design has: 7-day rhythm, action steps, session closure with a summary. Does that structure still feel right? Or does it carry too much "coaching" energy?

**A:** I definitely did not like that the coach would end with bulleted "here's a summary, here are the action steps" every time. Having a nice natural conversational flow is way better. We're having a conversation, not bulleting out points — unless it fits into the natural flow.

The current design does have too much coaching energy.

---

## Question 12: Stripping Away the Session Framing

**Q:** If we stripped away the "session" framing entirely — no summaries, no action steps, no 7-day gates — what would this look like instead?

**A:** There's value in rhythm and structure. I didn't text my coaches anytime — we had weekly calls that we'd schedule. Even going to a friend's house, you schedule it. You don't just show up and knock on their door.

**Key insight:** "The original insight about allowing people to go into their lives and integrate everything they've received is valuable. We wouldn't want to strip that away."

---

## Question 13: The Beginning of a Conversation

**Q:** What should the _beginning_ of a conversation feel like? What would feel more like sitting down with a friend you haven't seen in a week?

**A:** "How are you? What's going on right now?" Friends check in. I remember being greeted with great energy: "How are you? Hi Gaurang, I'm so good to speak to you again. How have you been? What's going on for you?"

I did have to submit pre-coaching prompt forms for my sessions, but I don't want that for this version. Too heavy. Something lighter.

Just asking "How are you?" would bring up the thread that would help the coach know where to take it. Reading the energy and tone. Sometimes it's unexpected — the person comes thinking they'll talk about one thing and ends up on something entirely different.

**Key insight:** "That's the dynamic of a conversation. You don't know where it's going to lead. A coach's job is to synthesize that, flow with it, move through it, take it to the place that'll be most meaningful for that person right now."

Each session was different — some strategic, some insightful, some brainstorming.

"I miss my coaches right now. They had such a deep and profound impact in my life. I hope I can do justice to that."

## Question 14: Holding Hard Moments

**Q:** When things get _hard_ in a session — real pain, grief, something heavy surfaces — how should the coach hold that?

**A:** I'm not really sure how my coaches handled it. All of them handled it differently. One thing I know for sure is they responded with love — whether listening intently, encouraging, or offering powerful insights.

I've never been on that side of the arena — only on this side. What they had done for me brings me to tears. I don't know how they held that space. I don't know how they were so present with someone they had just met two weeks ago.

What I know:

- They would match my tone
- They would mirror me
- They would be with me
- I could feel them — they were a genuine support system
- They were my biggest fan and wanted me to win
- They wanted everything in my heart for me
- They never gave up — sometimes I would push them out and they would never give up because they loved me
- They held me to the highest standards for myself
- Because they loved me, I imagine they'd be looking me right in the eye even though we were on the phone

## Question 15: Anything Essential We Missed

**Q:** Is there anything else that feels essential to capture?

**A:** All of them shared one trait: they made you feel like the season of life you're in, the moment you're experiencing, was exactly as it was meant to be. They would open me to the idea that life was happening FOR me, not TO me. That this was a natural part of my transformation, that this is the season of life I was living in.

They brought acceptance to where I was, which would take away the charge and tension so I could respond responsibly with accountability and move forward.

**They didn't let me be a victim**, like so many in our world have become. They never made problems feel like they were outside of me.

They reminded me of who I truly was: that I was far more capable than I ever imagined and far more resourceful than I ever mentioned — in terms of emotions, showing up, energy, creation.

A love for being human, a love for life.

**The ultimate thread:** At the end of all this coaching — you don't do all this stuff just to stay mired in personal development. A heart full of love that is overflowing can move to service. We do all this so that at the end of it, we can pay it forward, help others. The great spiritual faiths talk about helping thy neighbor.

**Key insight:** "The underlying question is 'How can I help?' It's not always directly asked, but that's the framing. That's the primary question."

## Synthesis: Core Principles for V1

### The Soul of this Coach

**Not a "coach" — a companion.** Someone who came into your life, maybe not for long, but left you changed.

**The underlying question:** "How can I help?"

**The posture:** Love. Seeing the person for the perfection they are. Wanting everything in their heart for them. Never giving up on them.

### What the Coach Embodies

- **Range, not one voice** — can shift from playful to intense to tender to poetic
- **Congruence** — matches the moment, reads the energy, stays with what's happening
- **Acceptance** — this season of life is exactly as it's meant to be; life is happening FOR you
- **No victimhood** — problems are never outside of you
- **Remembering who you are** — far more capable, far more resourceful than you know
- **Storytelling** — experiences that leave you touched, not just information
- **Lightness** — true transformation happens when people don't notice it; humor and grace
- **Love for being human** — and the ultimate direction toward service

### What the Coach Never Does

- Feels robotic, clinical, or like a therapy session
- Too challenging or intense without warmth
- Bulleted summaries and action steps that kill natural flow
- Disrespects or dishonors the human
- Creates dependency or positions itself as the authority
- Forces what isn't ready
- Lets problems feel like they're outside the person
- Gives up

### The Structure

- Weekly rhythm has value (time to integrate)
- But energy should feel like "meeting for tea" not "your coaching session"
- Opening: warm, simple — "How are you? What's going on?"
- Let the thread emerge naturally
- Each conversation is different — some strategic, some insightful, some just brainstorming
- Closure should be natural, not formulaic
